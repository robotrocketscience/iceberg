## Executive summary

Across all reactor-era cells and chunk sizes tested, **the architecture choice reduces to two decisions**:

1. **Single-stage all-electric vs two-stage with chemical Saturn-departure.** All-electric wins on raw delivered mass at every reactor power ≥ Fission Surface Power. Two-stage (chunk-fed chemical Variant B) only wins at Kilopower-era power constraint *and* only if the campaign optimizes net-present-value rather than raw delivered mass.

2. **What objective function the campaign is actually maximizing.** Raw delivered mass per ship and net-present-value per mission give different architecture answers at Kilopower era. The propulsion analysis cannot resolve this; it is a program-economic decision.

The carried-hypergolic two-stage architecture (chemical propellant launched from Earth) is dominated everywhere by either all-electric or Variant B once outbound launch mass is accounted for. It does not win a single cell.

**Important caveat on launch-mass numbers:** Every delivered-per-launch-mass ratio in this document was originally derived under an all-electric outbound assumption (specific impulse 2000 s, total outbound delta-v 9 km/s). The real concept of operations uses a chemical hydrolox trans-Saturn-injection kick stage, which imposes a constant **6.9× launch-mass multiplier** for mission 1 and roughly **1.3× multiplier for missions 2+** once a low Earth orbit depot is operational. The matrix's *relative ranking* survives intact; the *absolute economics* are 6.9× worse than the headline numbers suggest until depot infrastructure is built. See R-outbound-architecture for the per-cell impact.

**Conditional architecture flip — aerocapture (significantly weakened by R-chunk-as-heat-shield):** Earth aerocapture would collapse the matrix to "all-electric every era" *if it works for a 100-tonne fabric-bagged vehicle*. R-chunk-as-heat-shield established this is materially harder than the aerospace heritage suggests: ICEBERG's ballistic coefficient (4,000 kg/m²) is 40× higher than Mars Global Surveyor (~100 kg/m²), and there is **no altitude where both the bag survives AND aerobraking pass count is tractable**. The only viable atmospheric architectures require additional engineering — sacrificial bag + chunk-as-heat-shield, or a deployable inflatable drag skirt (ballute). See the "Aerocapture conditional architecture" section for the conditional matrix and the failure analysis. **The pre-aerocapture matrix below is the governing architecture unless one of those mitigations is engineered.**

## The three rules the matrix collapses to

### Rule 1: optimum reactor power tracks chunk mass, not specific power

From R-reactor-specific-power. The optimum reactor power is determined by the rocket-equation balance between chunk mass and the propellant fraction the electric stage must throw away. **Specific power is an independent multiplier on launch efficiency at every reactor power, not an optimum-shifter.**

Empirical relationship (from the sweep):

| Chunk | Optimum reactor power |
|---:|---:|
| 100 t | 40 kWe |
| 200 t | 100 kWe |
| 500 t | 200 kWe |
| 1000 t | 500 kWe (extrapolated) |

Roughly: optimum_reactor_kWe ≈ chunk_t × 0.4–0.5.

### Rule 2: carried-hypergolic two-stage is always worse

From R-two-stage-dv post-result correction and R-chunk-fed-chemical. The carried-hypergolic architecture pays outbound launch mass for ~89 tonnes of chemical propellant per 100-tonne chunk (at 2 km/s offload). That outbound penalty (~140 tonnes of low-Earth-orbit launch mass) is larger than the inbound reactor-mass savings.

In every cell tested across 24 reactor × chunk configurations, carried-hypergolic is dominated by either all-electric or Variant B. **Do not propose carried-hypergolic as an architecture option.**

### Rule 3: the 7-year burn cap is not load-bearing — it is an economic constraint disguised as a physical one

From R-bag-permeability-vs-burn-time. Prior conops documents used a 7-year inbound burn cap derived from the 14-year round-trip target. This cap forced Kilopower-era cells onto Variant B because all-electric burn times at Kilopower run 23–45 years.

The *physical* reason to cap inbound time is bag permeability (water vapor leak through bag walls during inbound coast). At the bag-engineering empirical permeability rate (0.65%/year), all-electric Kilopower delivers more raw mass than Variant B at every chunk size tested. Permeability up to 2.0%/year does not flip this for chunks ≤ 100 t.

**The 14-year ceiling is an economic preference (faster cycle, faster revenue) not a physical limit.** If the program accepts longer round-trips, the architecture matrix shifts toward all-electric across all eras.

**Addendum (post-evening):** Under continuous-thrust electric inbound, the practical specific-impulse optimum is at the *lower* band of the electric-propulsion envelope, not the upper. Higher specific impulse trades propellant economy for burn duration, which under L0-05 is the binding variable. This inverts the conventional ion-propulsion design intuition for time-constrained architectures and explains why titan's three narrowing constraints (high-elliptical departure, Isp 2000 s, chunk ≤ 200 t) all push *down* on the architecture rather than up.

### Rule 4: single-chunk delivered fraction rises with chunk mass (dry-mass amortisation)

From titan-2 R-multi-chunk-departure-orbit + R-HE-graze-feasibility cross-learning. Tug dry mass is roughly chunk-size-independent at fixed reactor power; per-mission delivered chunk mass is propellant-equation-bounded against that fixed dry mass. As chunk mass rises, the fixed dry mass amortises across more cargo, and the delivered fraction climbs.

Empirical relationship (B-ring-direct departure, 500 kilowatt-electric, Isp 2000 s, continuous-thrust electric inbound):

| Single-chunk mass | Delivered fraction |
|---:|---:|
| 50 tonnes | ~17 percent |
| 200 tonnes | ~17 percent (L1-007 cap) |
| 482 tonnes (B-ring single-chunk physical cap) | ~22 percent |
| asymptotic (chunk → infinity) | ~27 percent |

The matrix's bare "17 percent" delivered-fraction figure is anchored at the 200-tonne L1-007 cap. Up-cap scenarios (L1-007 relaxation toward 482 tonnes per ship) gain delivered fraction non-linearly — about 5 percentage points across the L1-007-to-physical-cap span, plus another ~5 points beyond physical cap if a multi-chunk-aggregation mode were physically accessible (it is not, per R-HE-graze-feasibility).

**Implication:** the L1-007 chunk-mass cap is doubly binding — it caps absolute delivered chunk mass AND caps delivered fraction. Relaxing L1-007 upward is a double lever, not single. The cap-vs-physical-cap gap is the largest single economic uplift available to the program short of a physics breakthrough.

**Multi-chunk under B-ring-direct (separate regime):** delivered fraction is ~11.4 percent asymptotic at 40.2 km/s inbound. Operates in a fundamentally different propulsive regime than single-chunk and would warrant its own matrix row if multi-chunk gets considered seriously despite R-HE-graze-feasibility's falsification.

The aerocapture conditional above was originally framed as a likely-to-close engineering question because Mars aerobraking has flight heritage. R-chunk-as-heat-shield established that the heritage does not transfer.

|---:|---:|---:|:--:|

**The vehicle's ballistic coefficient (4,000 kg/m²) is 40× higher than Mars Global Surveyor.** No altitude exists where bag survives AND pass count is tractable. The conditional is therefore harder than a "Mars aerobraking heritage applies" framing implied.

Three architectural mitigations remain plausible — each is its own engineering programme:

1. **Sacrificial bag + chunk-as-heat-shield.** Bag is retracted to the lee side before atmospheric entry; chunk presents ice face to flow; chunk surface ablates ~1 tonne (0.5% chunk loss) protecting whatever is behind it. 3. **Hybrid aerocapture-then-aerobraking.** Single-pass deep aerocapture with bag sacrificed during first pass, followed by multi-pass aerobraking with chunk-only-and-tug at higher altitude.

**None of these is a showstopper, but the conditional matrix above assumes one of them has been engineered.** Until one is qualified, the pre-aerocapture matrix is the governing architecture.

## How to size the reactor (operational decision rule)

For a target chunk size M_c tonnes at the inbound delta-v = 6.42 km/s residual:

1. **Pick architecture from the matrix above** based on era and objective function.
2. **Pick electric specific impulse** from the matrix (1500–5000 s; 2000 s is the most common winner).
3. **Compute optimum reactor power**: ≈ 0.45 × M_c kilowatt-electric.
4. **Check schedule feasibility**: burn time should fit your round-trip target. If not, either bump electric specific impulse down (faster burn, less efficient), reduce chunk size, or accept longer round-trip.
5. **Check launch mass under realistic outbound architecture**:
   - Vehicle dry mass at 10 watts-per-kilogram = 5 t tug + (reactor kWe × 0.1) tonnes reactor.
   - **Mission 1 (chemical-kick outbound, no depot yet):** multiply by ~11 for low Earth orbit launch mass. Verify this is within the lift capability of your launch vehicle or budget for on-orbit assembly.
   - **Mission 2+ (depot operational):** multiply by ~2 for marginal Earth-launch mass. The remainder is depot-sourced kick propellant.
   - **All-electric outbound (alternative, no kick stage):** multiply by 1.583. Pays Van Allen radiation cost (extra shielding mass not in this rule) and reactor cycle-life cost (extra outbound on-time) not in this rule.

For Variant B (Kilopower NPV case): same as above plus add ~10 tonnes for the electrolyzer + cryotankage + chemical stage dry; chemical propellant comes from chunk at Saturn (no outbound chemical-propellant penalty).

## Decision points the program needs to make

These are not propulsion decisions. They set the architecture indirectly by setting the objective function.

1. **Discount rate / objective function.** Raw delivered mass or net-present-value? If NPV, what discount rate? This sets Kilopower-era architecture (all-electric vs Variant B) and influences whether the 14-yr ceiling is treated as binding.

2. **14-yr round-trip ceiling.** Sacrosanct or relaxable? If relaxed to ≥25 yr, Kilopower all-electric becomes the natural choice on raw-mass; if held, Variant B is the Kilopower architecture.

3. **Reactor specific-power investment.** A 40 W/kg program at sub-megawatt power is roughly equivalent to a 10 W/kg megawatt program on launch efficiency. The first is a different program with different cost / risk / timeline than the second. Which is the campaign's bet?

4. **Bag-laminate qualification.** Empirical permeability is 0.65%/year. The architecture matrix is robust to permeability up to ~1.5%/year. If the bag underperforms (≥ 2%/year), Variant B's winning region expands meaningfully.

5. **Aerocapture R&D — deprioritized.** After R-chunk-as-heat-shield + R-deployable-drag-skirt, no near-term path to closing aerocapture for ICEBERG's ballistic coefficient and entry velocity. Aerocapture moved from "highest-leverage unresolved" to "long-horizon upside contingent on thermal-protection-system breakthrough." Decision: do not fund aerocapture R&D as part of the ICEBERG critical path.

6. **Low Earth orbit depot architecture and financing.** Mission 2+ economics depend on a cryogenic hydrolox depot existing. That depot is a multi-billion-dollar program in its own right (build cost not in any of these rounds; boiloff and operating cost likewise excluded). Without it, every ICEBERG mission carries 6.9× the launch mass of the prior matrix's headline numbers. The depot is the structural lever that turns ICEBERG from a one-shot 1000-tonne launch event into a 50-tonne-per-mission steady state.

7. **Mission-1 capital amortization.** Mission 1 launches under chemical-kick outbound with no depot. Per-mission launch mass is 175–1147 tonnes depending on cell. The R-NPV finding of 4–7% project internal-rate-of-return was conditional on all-electric outbound; the real number, with mission 1 amortized over steady-state revenue, is meaningfully lower. The program's financial structure must distinguish one-shot mission-1 capital from recurring mission-2+ operating cost.

8. **Year-twenty-plus chunk-operations altitude (deferred — no surviving year-twenty-plus cell).** B-ring (status quo, simpler bag mechanics, was incompatible with all-electric end-to-end) vs high-elliptical (engineering change to bag and capture mechanism, was compatible with all-electric end-to-end). This decision sets the conops-phase-5/6 re-design scope **if** R-chunk-as-heat-shield-revisit closes and reactivates a year-twenty-plus cell. Currently moot.

9. **Megawatt-reactor program path (new — load-bearing).** The surviving 500-kWe chemical-kick cell requires a reactor 5× FSP Phase 2's scope. FSP Phase 2 has not been awarded; FY2026 budget zeroed NEP/NTP lines; DRACO cancelled. Per the 0-of-6 base-rate prior, posterior probability of available 500-kWe reactor by 2032–2035 is 0.10–0.30. Decision: (a) fund or co-fund a reactor program parallel to ICEBERG with explicit scope expansion past FSP Phase 2, (b) accept a non-fission baseline (radioisotope, solar-electric at Saturn distance is infeasible; chemical-only requires retire of all-electric concept), or (c) accept that ICEBERG cannot launch within the demonstrator window and reframe as a longer-horizon technology program.

10. **L0-10 reliability target and redundancy budget (new — coordinated with REQUIREMENTS amendment).** Per enceladus's four rounds: keep L0-10 at 0.90 (Option A) requires $565M–$710M per-vehicle redundancy budget; relax L0-10 alone (Option B/C) is illusory because L0-09 charitable readings bind tighter; coordinated revision (relax both L0-09 and L0-10 to 0.80) at launch cadence ≥ 2/yr requires per-mission p ≥ 0.93 and saves the redundancy overlay. **Project-owner-confirmed in REQUIREMENTS.md v0.6:** coordinated revision (Option B+coordinated-L0-09) at 2/yr cadence floor.

11. **Chemical-trim-vs-all-electric Earth arrival (RESOLVED).** Per rhea-2 Round 3 — chemical-trim is worse than all-electric, not better. Project-owner-locked Option A: Variant B restated as 500-kilowatt-electric all-electric inbound at 17 percent delivered.
