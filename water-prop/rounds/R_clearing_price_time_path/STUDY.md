**Round:** R-clearing-price-time-path. SCOPE pre-registered 2026-07-21. All four hypotheses **HELD**. This round supplies the anchor R183 said it lacked — and the anchored value sits deep inside R183's kill zone.

## Results vs registered hypotheses — all four HELD

### H1 — the LEO clearing-price anchor — **HELD**

Water/propellant in LEO clears at **$3,000–4,000/kg** in 2025 (central $3,500): Earth launch $3,868/kg, propellant-to-LEO ~$4,000/kg, lunar propellant at a 25 % concession ~$3,000/kg. The independent ISRU cross-check (25 % off $3,868) lands at **$2,901 ≈ the band floor** — two derivations agree within 3 %.

### H2 — the decline rate, anchored — **HELD**

The launch-cost experience curve ($3,868/kg 2025 → $273/kg 2040 median; −75 % by 2040 conservative) implies a delivered-water price decline of **9.2 %/yr (conservative) to 17.7 %/yr (median)**, geometric-central **12.8 %/yr** (bull case 32 %/yr, treated as an outlier). **R183's kill threshold was 3 %/yr.** The empirical rate is **3.1–5.9× the rate that already sinks the waiver's NPV case.** R183 swept this parameter open and happened to draw its illustrative "decay kills fast" example at 3 % — the real curve is far steeper.

### H3 — the timeline multiplier and the effective rate — **HELD**

Over the mission's own timeline the substitute price collapses: **3.3× (13 yr, conservative) to 63× (23.5 yr, median)**. At the central rate, water delivered at the end of R183's 23.5-yr paper timeline clears at **5.0 % of its decision-time value**; over the 13-yr round trip alone, **19 %**. The decisive structural fact: **the decline rate alone (12.8 %/yr) exceeds both campaign hurdles (8 %, 10 %).** The combined effective revenue discount (hurdle + decline ≈ 21–23 %/yr) drives the 23.5-yr revenue discount factor to **0.76 % (8 % hurdle) / 0.47 % (10 %)**. The market discounts ICEBERG's revenue faster than capital does; a zero-WACC, instantly-built ICEBERG still loses because every year's water is worth less than last year's and the substitute keeps undercutting it.

### H4 — the surviving niche, and the dilemma — **HELD**

The one clearing-price regime immune to the LEO curve is a **deep-space** destination, where Earth launch pays the full rocket-equation penalty: at Δv {10, 15, 20} km/s the substitute is **9.6× / 29.9× / 93× LEO** (~$34 k / $105 k / $325 k per kg). But that market needs outer-system demand that does not exist in the 2032–2050 window and is itself gated on cheap launch. **The dilemma is structural: ICEBERG's water is either sold cheap (LEO, into a substitute racing away at ~13 %/yr) or dear (deep space, to customers who do not exist).** This does not falsify the engineering — the locked matrix verdict brackets economics by construction — but it names the bet the engineering sits on.

## Revisit (mandatory)

Externally anchored **desk estimates, not a market study** — the bands are the claim. Load-bearing simplifications: (1) clearing price = substitute price (ICEBERG is a price-taker with no premium; a strategic/sovereign buyer, or supply constraints on lunar ISRU, could sustain a premium — unmodeled and speculative). (2) Experience curves flatten as they mature; the PNAS Nexus curve already embeds some flattening, but a hard post-2040 plateau would blunt the tail (it does not rescue the 2025–2040 leg, which alone falls 9–18 ×). (3) The deep-space substitute uses chemical Isp 450 s; a high-Isp Earth-launched tug lowers that multiplier and *worsens* ICEBERG's niche. (4) No demand model — the "no outer-system customers" claim is a window judgment, not a forecast. (5) The 23.5-yr timeline is R183's paper figure; a faster program shortens the decay leg but not below the ~13-yr physics round trip. A full-cashflow round (R15-rerun lineage, with NRE, sovereign-purchase scenarios, and a real demand curve) is the honest next step and is named, not run.
