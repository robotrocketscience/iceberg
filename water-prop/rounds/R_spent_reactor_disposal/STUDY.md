# R-spent-reactor-disposal — STUDY

**Round:** R-spent-reactor-disposal. SCOPE pre-registered 2026-07-21. All four hypotheses **HELD**. A lean round with a clean verdict: disposal is cheap and precedented — **not a binding bet** — but it imposes three design constraints, one of them unpriced.

## Results vs registered hypotheses — all four HELD

### H1 — disposal Δv, and the R186 asymmetry — **HELD**

Controlled deorbit of a co-orbital B-ring reactor into Saturn's atmosphere is **2.78 km/s** (lower periapsis to atmosphere-grazing, apoapsis at the ring). From a Titan-crossing staging orbit the periapsis-lowering is **0.52 km/s** — inside a single Titan flyby's **1.23 km/s** capacity, so **disposal is free via one flyby**, exactly the Cassini "goodbye kiss" method. The registered asymmetry with R186 holds: **Titan can lower periapsis (disposal, free) but cannot raise v∞ (departure, capped ~3.3 km/s)**. The well helps going down, not up — the same body that couldn't buy the departure is a free disposal lever.

### H2 — inventory and deorbit mass — **HELD**

U-235 per Kilopower unit is **27.7 kg**, and it is barely touched: operational burnup over ~3 fpy at ~500 kWth is **0.58 kg**. The disposal hazard is the fission-product inventory and neutron activation, not fuel depletion — the core comes home (or is left at Saturn) still carrying essentially its full HEU load. Deorbiting a spent core **parked co-orbital** (rather than on a Titan orbit) costs **43 % of reactor mass** in MET propellant: **6.6 t** at paper κ (16 t reactor) to **27.5 t** at flown κ (65 t reactor). The lesson: park spent cores where Titan can reach them, and disposal is free; park them co-orbital, and you pay a real propellant tax that scales with the specific-power bet.

### H3 — planetary protection — **HELD**

Enceladus (global subsurface ocean, active south-polar plumes) and Titan (organics, subsurface water) make the Saturn system COSPAR-governed. The Cassini precedent is the bar: NASA deliberately plunged Cassini into Saturn in 2017 rather than accept even a **~1-in-10⁶** chance of striking Enceladus or Titan. Anything left at Saturn requires the same controlled, high-probability atmospheric disposal. The **monolithic's returning reactor moves the concern to cislunar** — an activated fission core arriving in the delivery zone is a distinct, and arguably harder, regulatory/political problem than a clean Saturn plunge.

### H4 — cheap and precedented, but constraining — **HELD**

Disposal is **not a binding bet**: ≤ 2.8 km/s direct (a third of the departure budget), free via Titan, and demonstrated by Cassini. But it imposes three constraints: **(a)** the reactor must be **separable and independently disposable** — disposal cannot depend on a mothership that may not survive or return; **(b)** the fleet must **park spent cores on a Titan-crossing orbit and reserve one disposal flyby each**, or pay the 43%-of-mass co-orbital deorbit tax; **(c)** the monolithic delivers an **activated reactor to cislunar** — an **unpriced** delivery-zone planetary-protection and nuclear-safety constraint. Constraint (c) is the sharp one and the campaign has not touched it.

## Revisit (mandatory)

The fission-product activity time-profile is not computed (the round bounds the *fuel* inventory and cites the disposal *method*, not the source term); a real PP case needs the shutdown activity and its decay, plus the probability-of-Enceladus-contamination calculation Cassini actually did. The 43%-of-mass deorbit figure uses MET at 800 s; a small dedicated solid/chemical de-orbit stage would change the mass accounting (and might be lighter for a one-shot burn). The cislunar activated-reactor constraint (c) is named, not analyzed — it is the honest follow-on and could be materially limiting (launch licensing for a fuelled reactor, and re-entry safety of a returning activated core are both real regulatory gates, per the NASA/DoE launch-approval history for RTG missions). Whether a spent core can even be safely handled for a controlled cislunar disposal vs. a targeted heliocentric-graveyard injection is unexamined.
